Question
What is your stand on this verse considering that Jesus (A.S.) is a God in the Bible and in the Qur’an he is a Prophet?
Answer
Assalamu `Alaikum! Let me elaborate this question for us to understand the scope of our topic. The previous scriptures mentioned in the verse refer to the Torah (part of the Jewish Bible) and the Gospels, both part of the Christian Bible. From the above verse, it says that if we Muslims are in doubt of the revelations in the Qur'an, we must therefore refer to the previous scriptures for verification. Further, since the previous scripture is part of the Christian Bible, then the above question simply states that we Muslim must acknowledge the deity of Jesus (A.S.) because that's what the Bible says according to the Church.

With that in mind, we can say that Jesus (A.S.) plays a pivotal role on the two religions (Islam and Christianity), and we can briefly summarize their differences as follows:

The trinitarian Christians believe that Jesus (A.S.) is a God and is part of the triune God. They claim that God is 3 in 1, that is the God the Father, the God the Son, and the Holy Ghost are all equal and one in essence. On the other hand, we Muslims refer to Jesus (A.S.) as prophet and rejects his divinity and the concept of triune God as claimed by the trinitarians. Instead, Muslims believe in pure monotheism (absolute oneness of God).

Having laid out the belief of the two parties with regards to Jesus (A.S.), we can now address the question starting with the context of the verse. Consider the following verses, 10:88-93:

Upon reading, we can safely say that in context, Allah (S.W.T.) is referring to the time of Moses (A.S.) during the tyranny of the Pharaoh. That is, the previous scriptures being referred in 10:94 is the Torah and not the Gospels. The 10:94, however, can be generalized to accomodate the latter revelation. Hence at this point, we need to ask the following question as well:

Does the Qur'an acknowledges the Torah, the Gospels and all the prophets (from Adam to Abraham, to Noah, to Moses, to Jesus, to Muhammad [may God's peace and blessings be with them all])?

The answer is Yes, and we can support this with several verses such as:

At this point, it is already clear from the above evidence that we Muslim acknowledges all previous scriptures given to the prophets. The problem is, if we Muslim must refer to the previous scriptures and ask the Christians, they would say that Allah (S.W.T.) is not the only God, but rather He is part of the triune God with the inclusion of Jesus (A.S.) and the Holy Spirit. What a huge contradiction!

How can Allah (S.W.T.) repeatedly with great warning, explicitly says that He has no partners and is absolutely One in the Quran, and yet command us to ask those reading the previous scriptures (i.e. Jewish and Christians), in which a party of them (i.e. the trinitarian Christians) claimed that He is part of the triune God?

To answer this question, remember that the Qur'an was revealed after the Bible, and the purpose of this revelation is stated in the following verses:

For example, according to the new testament of the Christian bible, it says that God has taken a son, but the Qur'an rejects this.

Notice how the verse commenced, "They have said ...". This phrase simply proves how Qur'an looks at the previous scriptures, it tries to confirm and correct the mistakes done. Why, well obviously we were not alive in that time, and as the verse states "... Do you say about Allah that which you do not know?". This is to counter the argument made by the trinitarians, that if the previous scripture is wrong and the Qur'an confirms those scriptures, then the Qur'an is also wrong. Therefore, if we use the Qur'an as a criterion, how do we solve the contradiction stated in the above Question? Well, the answer is the following verse:
